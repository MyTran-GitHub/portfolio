The honest number matters

A naïve transfer would greenlight AWD across 62% of Japan. Account for topographic slope and field fragmentation, and only 18% is truly feasible — a 3.4× overestimation, with extension costs 3.8× higher on the fragmented terrain that remains. Knowing where not to deploy is as valuable as knowing where to.

Why the naïve model overestimated by 3.4×

Water deficit alone doesn't make a paddy AWD-suitable. How you count that deficit does — and the standard model counts it the wrong way.

THE PROBLEM · NELSON–SANDER DEKAD COUNTING

Every deficit counted the same

The water balance for each dekad (10-day period) is WB = P − (PET + Percolation) — rainfall minus evapotranspiration and soil percolation. The standard model just flags WB < 0 as "a suitable drying dekad," whether the shortfall is −5 mm or −150 mm.

That's the bug: a −150 mm dekad doesn't mean gentler drying, it means the field has blown past the soil-moisture threshold into crop stress — the opposite of AWD-suitable. Counting it as a win is what inflates the map to 62% of Japan.

THE FIX · CUSTOM PYTHON + GOOGLE EARTH ENGINE

A threshold, not a coin flip

We rebuilt the dekad-level water balance in Earth Engine — CHIRPS daily rainfall summed to dekads, MODIS MOD16A2 evapotranspiration reprojected to the 500m study grid, percolation from SoilGrids texture class — then reclassified deficit against a −50mm safe-drying ceiling instead of a bare zero.

A dekad only counts as suitable if the deficit occurs and stays inside that ceiling. Re-run at that threshold, the map drops from 62% to a defensible 32% before biophysical constraints are even applied.
