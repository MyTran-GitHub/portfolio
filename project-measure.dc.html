Why vision kept changing

Three architectures, three different failure modes — not a straight line to the "best" model, but a record of what the previous one got wrong.

PHASE 1 · CNN CLASSIFICATION

ResNet-50 hits a wall

A whole-image classifier can say a tile is "urban" — it can't say where the block ends and the shadow begins. ResNet-50 kept losing the boundary between neighbourhoods whenever a cloud shadow crossed it, because classification throws away exactly the spatial structure a policy map needs.

The model is noticeably less confident on low-wealth residential blocks — F1 of 0.79 in SF, 0.72 in Seoul — mistaking them for industrial zones ~1 in 5 times.

PHASE 2 · U-NET SEGMENTATION

Trading a label for a map

The fix wasn't a bigger classifier — it was a different question. U-Net predicts a class for every pixel, so it preserves the physical geometry of a block instead of collapsing it into one tag. That dense, pixel-level view is what let us measure inequality by neighbourhood rather than by city.

▹ 95% mIoU on held-out tiles
Tree canopy tells the story in both cities — 35%→12% across wealthy-to-poor San Francisco blocks, a nearly-as-wide 28%→9% in Seoul — while building footprint runs the other way, denser in poor neighbourhoods (72%) than wealthy ones (48%).

PHASE 3 · VISION TRANSFORMERS

Letting attention find the pattern

A U-Net still looks at a fixed neighbourhood of pixels. Self-attention doesn't — it lets any patch of the image relate to any other, across all 13 spectral bands at once. That's what surfaces morphology a convolution misses: a corridor of heat that only reads as a pattern from across the whole city.
